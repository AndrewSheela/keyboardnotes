Relative Scales

Understanding how Major and Minor scales are connected

What Are Relative Scales?

Every Major scale has a relative Minor scale, and every Minor scale has a relative Major scale.

Relative scales use the same notes and share the same key signature, but they begin on a different starting note.

The Relationship Rule

The relative minor of any Major scale always starts on the 6th note of the Major scale.

This change of starting point alters the mood and emotional color of the music — even though the notes remain the same.

Example

C Major → Relative Minor → A Minor

Both scales contain the same notes: C D E F G A B

The only difference is the note we treat as the home note (tonic).

Why Relative Scales Matter

Relative scales help musicians switch moods without changing key signatures.

They are essential for songwriting, improvisation, and understanding music theory clearly.

Mastering relative scales makes music feel logical instead of confusing.
